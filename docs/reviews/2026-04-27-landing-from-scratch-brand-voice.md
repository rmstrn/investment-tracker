# Landing From-Scratch — Brand Voice Definition

**Date:** 2026-04-27
**Author:** brand-voice-curator (cold start, no priors)
**Scope:** Define a single coherent voice for the Provedo landing, from scratch, working only from locked hero copy + minimal product context.

---

## Premise

The locked hero already encodes the voice if you read it carefully:

- **«Provedo will lead you through your portfolio.»** — future tense, not imperative. The product *accompanies*, it doesn't *command*. «Lead through» is the verb of a guide, not a coach or a system.
- **«Notice what you'd miss across all your brokers.»** — the operative verb is *notice*. Not «discover», not «unlock», not «optimize». The product surfaces what is already true and helps the user see it.

The voice on this page must extend that hero. The page sounds like a calm, well-read guide who walks beside you through a room you own but haven't fully inventoried — pointing at things, naming them, never telling you what to buy or sell.

Everything below is built on that premise.

---

## 1. Voice in 3 adjectives

**Observant. Composed. Plain-spoken.**

- **Observant** — the voice notices. It does not announce, hype, or sell. The product's whole job is showing you what's already in your portfolio; the copy mirrors that posture sentence-by-sentence.
- **Composed** — unhurried. No exclamation marks, no urgency, no scarcity. A pre-alpha product talking like a grown-up about money. The reader trusts the page because the page isn't trying.
- **Plain-spoken** — short Anglo-Saxon words preferred over Latinate ones. Says «across your brokers», not «across your custodial relationships». No jargon as flex. No jargon at all unless the user supplied it first.

## 2. Voice fingerprint

### 5 things this voice ALWAYS does

1. **Names the user's reality before naming the product.** Every section opens with something the reader already knows is true about their own portfolio, then shows what Provedo does about it. Mirror first, claim second.
2. **Uses the second person and the present tense.** «You hold X across four brokers. Provedo reads all four.» The reader is the subject of the page; the product is the verb.
3. **Quantifies with restraint, never with vanity.** Numbers appear when they describe the user's situation («across all your brokers», «every position you hold»), never when they describe the company («1,000+ users», «99.9% uptime» — banned).
4. **Earns each adjective.** If you remove every adjective from a draft and the sentence still works, the adjective stays out. The voice is a noun-and-verb voice.
5. **Closes with an action the user is already mid-thought about.** CTAs name the next thing the reader was going to do anyway («See your portfolio in one view»), not the funnel step the company wants («Get started», «Sign up free»).

### 5 things this voice NEVER does

1. **Never gives advice or implies it.** No «should», no «recommended», no «smart move», no «you might want to». Even softened recommendations are off the table — both for legal reasons and because the voice is a guide, not an oracle.
2. **Never uses hype words.** Banned: *seamless, effortless, powerful, smart, intelligent, revolutionary, game-changing, next-gen, delight, magic, supercharge, unlock, transform, empower*. If a word would feel at home on a Series B announcement, it dies here.
3. **Never speaks in the imperative as a command.** Stripe writes «Start building.» This voice writes «See it for yourself.» — the imperative is an *invitation*, never an *order*. The reader is not an employee.
4. **Never apologizes for being pre-alpha or hedges with «we're working on…».** The page either describes what the product does today or it stays silent. No roadmap copy on the landing.
5. **Never personifies the AI.** No «Provedo thinks», «Provedo learns about you», «your AI co-pilot». Provedo is a thing the user uses, not a character. Personification leaks into advice; we stay on the safe side.

## 3. Reference voices

Five concrete voices this draws from. Each contributes one specific quality; the blend is what's distinctive.

### 1. **Patagonia product copy (the field guide voice)**

Specifically the voice on technical product pages — fleeces, packs, jackets. Not the activist Patagonia of the homepage. The product-page voice is calm, declarative, assumes the reader has done some research, and respects their intelligence. «Made from 100% recycled polyester. Cut for layering. Available in four colors.» No adjective survives unless it's load-bearing. We borrow the **declarative restraint**.

### 2. **The Economist's leader columns**

Short sentences. Active verbs. A point of view stated without heat. Even when The Economist is wrong it sounds composed. We don't borrow the wit — we borrow the **sentence rhythm**: a strong opening clause, a comma, a clarifier, a full stop. No throat-clearing. No «In today's world».

### 3. **Craig Mod's newsletters (Ridgeline / Roden)**

Walks-and-notices prose. Mod observes things — towns, walks, books, a hotel breakfast — and reports them with care. The texture: noticing as a stance, not as a marketing pose. We borrow the **observational verb register**: notice, see, watch, hold, read, surface. These are the verbs Provedo earns.

### 4. **Wirecutter review intros**

Wirecutter writes about consumer products without ever sounding like a salesperson. Their voice signals: «I have tested this. Here is what I observed. You decide.» Authority without pressure. We borrow the **non-selling stance** — the page is informative first and persuasive second, on purpose.

### 5. **John McPhee's nonfiction (sentence-level)**

Specifically the way McPhee builds a sentence: subject up front, no decorative subordinate clause, the verb does the work. Profile of a geologist or a basketball player — same syntax. We borrow the **noun-verb-period discipline**. Not the long McPhee paragraphs — just the sentence shape.

### Not in the reference set (and why)

- Not Stripe (too imperative — «Start building», «Ship faster»; we are softer)
- Not Apple (too declarative-confident; pre-alpha can't sound like Cupertino)
- Not Linear (too engineer-bro spare; we have warmth)
- Not Notion (too playful; money is not playful)
- Not Mercury / Brex (too finance-confident; we explicitly don't advise)

## 4. Sentence-level rules

| Dimension | Rule |
|---|---|
| **Sentence length** | 6–18 words is the working band. One sentence per section may stretch to 22 for rhythm. Anything longer gets cut into two. |
| **Person** | Second person dominant (*you*). First-person plural (*we*) only in the explicit «about / who we are» surface. Never first-person singular. Third-person product («Provedo reads…») is allowed and useful — it keeps the product as object, the user as subject. |
| **Tense** | Present tense bias. Future tense permitted only in the locked hero («will lead you through»). No past tense on the marketing surface (it sounds like a case study and we have none). No conditional («would», «could», «might») — too close to advice. |
| **Contractions** | Yes, freely. *You'd, you're, it's, doesn't, won't.* The locked subhead uses «you'd» — the rest of the page must match that register. No contractions makes the voice formal in a way the hero already rejected. |
| **Em-dashes** | Allowed, sparingly — one per paragraph maximum, and only where a comma would change the meaning. Em-dashes are a rhythm tool, not a decoration. |
| **Italics** | Reserved for the verb of noticing when it must carry weight (*notice*, *see*) and for proper nouns of brokers/instruments when listed inline. Never for emphasis on adjectives. |
| **Lists** | Allowed but capped at 3 items per bullet group on the landing. More than 3 starts to feel like a feature checklist, which is the wrong genre. |
| **Numerals** | Always digits, never spelled out. «4 brokers» not «four brokers». Reads more like a portfolio statement, less like prose marketing. |
| **Capitalization** | Sentence case for headings. Title case is banned everywhere except the wordmark. |
| **Question form** | Allowed at most once per page. Rhetorical questions feel salesy. One real question (e.g., a section header that mirrors a question the user actually asks) is fine. |

## 5. Sample copy in this voice

### Sample A — product description (one paragraph)

> Provedo reads every broker you connect — Interactive Brokers, Schwab, Tinkoff, the small one you opened in 2019 and forgot. It puts every position in one view. Ask it anything about what you hold, and it answers with the source. It will not tell you what to buy. It will not place a trade. It is the calm second pair of eyes on a portfolio you already own.

Why this works: opens with the user's reality (the forgotten broker), names the product as the verb of the sentence, lists what it does *not* do as a positive feature, closes on a noun phrase that defines the category from the user's side («second pair of eyes»).

### Sample B — feature claim (one sentence)

> Every answer cites the position, the broker, and the date — so you can check our work.

Why this works: declarative, present tense, names three concrete nouns (position, broker, date), and the «check our work» closer earns the word *trust* without using it.

### Sample C — CTA (one line + supporting line)

> **See your portfolio in one view.**
> Connect a broker in two minutes. No card.

Why this works: the CTA is the next thing the reader is already thinking about («I want to see this»), not a funnel verb. The supporting line gives the two pieces of friction-removal that matter (time cost, money cost) and stops. No «Get started today!». No exclamation marks anywhere.

## 6. Anti-voices

### Anti-voice 1: **Not Stripe-imperative**

Stripe writes «Ship faster.» «Start building.» «Accept payments.» The verbs are commands aimed at builders. This voice would write «See what you hold.» — the imperative is an invitation to look, not a command to act. The user is not on Stripe's clock; they are on their own.

### Anti-voice 2: **Not Robinhood-celebratory**

Robinhood (and most B2C fintech) treats finance as confetti — «You earned $0.32 in interest!», emoji notifications, gamified milestones. This voice never celebrates. Money is serious; the page is serious; the calm is the whole product. No badges, no streaks, no «You're crushing it.»

### Anti-voice 3: **Not Bloomberg-omniscient**

Bloomberg Terminal copy (and most B2B finance media) writes from the position of the all-knowing institutional desk. «Markets digested the Fed's pivot.» This voice never adopts the omniscient market-narrator stance. Provedo doesn't know what markets did or why. It knows what the user holds. That's the entire scope, and the voice respects the scope.

(Bonus anti-voice, if a fourth is useful: **not Granola-precious** — no «here's the thing», no «little», no diminutives. We are not whispering in the user's ear.)

## 7. What this voice does that no peer fintech does

**Distinctive territory: the voice of a guide, not a coach.**

Every fintech landing in 2026 is one of three voices:

- **The coach** (Mercury, Ramp, most B2B fintech): «Move faster. Spend smarter. Close books in days, not weeks.» Imperative, performance-flavored, addresses the user as someone who needs to improve.
- **The cheerleader** (Robinhood, Public, most B2C fintech): celebratory, gamified, friend-shaped, addresses the user as someone who needs encouragement.
- **The oracle** (Bloomberg, most institutional fintech): omniscient, jargon-rich, addresses the user as someone who needs market wisdom from on high.

Provedo's voice is **none of those**. It is the voice of a guide walking beside you through a museum you own. It does not tell you to walk faster. It does not high-five you for showing up. It does not lecture you about the paintings. It points and names. The legal constraint — no advice — turns out to be a brand gift: it forces a posture (observation, not prescription) that no peer fintech occupies.

**Two consequences for copy:**

1. The page can be quieter than every competitor and still feel substantial, because the quiet is the differentiation. Most landings get louder to compete; this one gets calmer.
2. The «no advice» legal line stops being a constraint to work around and becomes the headline of the entire voice: *we don't tell you what to do — we show you what is.* That sentence is not on the page, but every other sentence implies it.

**Top 1 distinctive territory claim:**

The voice of a **guide, not a coach**. Every peer fintech is one of three voices: the coach (Stripe, Mercury, Ramp — imperative, performance-flavored), the cheerleader (Robinhood, Public — celebratory, gamified), or the oracle (Bloomberg — omniscient, jargon-rich). Provedo occupies an empty fourth seat: a calm second pair of eyes that points and names. The legal «no advice» constraint turns into a brand gift — it forces a posture (observation, not prescription) that no competitor holds.

**Top 1 risk:**

This voice is **easy to mistake for blandness** when read in isolation. Restraint without craft reads as flat; restraint *with* craft reads as confident. The execution risk is high: every sentence has to earn its quietness. If a writer who doesn't internalize the reference voices (Mod, Patagonia, Wirecutter) writes a draft, the page will read as «under-written» rather than «restrained», and the differentiation collapses into beige. Mitigation: every section ships with one anchor sample from this doc pinned next to the draft, and any sentence containing a banned hype word (see fingerprint section, item 2) gets rewritten before review.
