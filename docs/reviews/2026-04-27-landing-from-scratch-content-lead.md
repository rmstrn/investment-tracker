# Landing copy direction — from scratch

**Author:** content-lead
**Date:** 2026-04-27
**Brief:** New landing for Provedo (chat-first portfolio AI, pre-alpha). Hero head + sub LOCKED. Everything else open.
**Status:** editorial direction (not final copy lock)

---

## 0. Read this first

The current landing has been polished too many times by too many hands. That is the problem. Every sentence has been "improved" until it carries no weight. The page reads like a product description written by a committee that respects every stakeholder and trusts no reader.

The fix is not better adjectives. The fix is **fewer sentences, each one earning its place**. A reader landing here has 14 seconds before they decide whether to keep scrolling. The current copy spends those seconds explaining what we are. We need to spend them on something the reader actually wants.

What does this reader want? Not "AI portfolio tracker." They want **the relief of finally being able to ask a question and get a real answer about their own money** — without 90 minutes of CSV-wrangling, four browser tabs, and a vague feeling that they are missing something important.

The page should sound like that relief is one click away. Quietly confident. No selling.

---

## 1. The one sentence the page must earn

After the locked hero, the entire page exists to earn this one line:

> **Your portfolio finally has someone who reads it as carefully as you do — and answers when you ask.**

This is the line a reader will paraphrase to a friend. Everything else on the page is in service of making this credible. If a section does not make this line more believable, that section gets cut.

Why this line works:
- "Finally" carries the reader's existing frustration without naming it (no "tired of CSVs?" condescension).
- "Reads it as carefully as you do" reframes AI from "robot doing things" to "second pair of eyes" — non-threatening, advice-adjacent without crossing the line.
- "Answers when you ask" is the entire product in five words: chat-first, on-demand, not push-notification-spam.
- It does not promise returns, performance, or insight quality. It promises **attention** — which is what the product actually delivers.

---

## 2. Reading mode the page assumes

**Both, in two layers.**

- **Scanner layer (90% of visitors):** can absorb the entire value prop from headers + visuals + CTAs alone, in under 20 seconds. If they only read the bold text, they should still understand: what this is, who it is for, why it is different, what to do next.
- **Reader layer (10% who care):** body copy rewards stopping. Each section has one sentence that earns the scroll, and at most three sentences of context. No paragraph blocks. No 80-word marketing prose.

Practically: every section is **header (scannable) + 1 hero sentence (the earner) + 2-3 short supporting lines (for the reader)**. Density is calibrated so the page can be skimmed in 20 seconds or read in 3 minutes.

What this means for copy:
- Headers do real work. They are not decoration.
- No "intro paragraphs" before the actual point.
- Body copy is sentence-per-line whitespace, not block prose.

---

## 3. Tone in 3 adjectives

1. **Composed** — like the friend who has been investing for 20 years and never raises their voice. Not loud. Not clever. Not selling. They just know.
2. **Specific** — concrete nouns over abstract claims. "Your Schwab dividend on March 14" beats "comprehensive portfolio insights." Specificity is the fastest way to earn trust without testimonials.
3. **Quietly bilingual** — the English voice and the Russian voice should feel like the same person speaking two languages, not two translations stapled together. Both should sound like an adult talking to an adult. Russian uses imperative mood naturally ("спроси", "посмотри"); English avoids the Stripe-style "Build. Ship. Scale." imperative and prefers second-person declarative ("You can ask...").

What this is **not**: not warm, not playful, not "we get you," not "your AI co-pilot." Those tones have been used up.

## 4. Section-by-section copy direction

The page is **6 sections**. Not 9. Not 12. Six. Each one earns its position or it gets cut.

### Section 1 — Hero (LOCKED)

**Eyebrow (proposed, above headline):**
EN: `Portfolio intelligence, on demand.`
RU: `Понимание портфеля по запросу.`

The eyebrow does one job: tell a scanner what category this is, before they read the headline. "Portfolio intelligence" positions us above "tracker" without claiming "advisor." "On demand" signals chat-first without saying "AI."

**Headline (LOCKED):**
EN: `Provedo will lead you through your portfolio.`
RU: `Provedo проведёт тебя по твоему портфелю.`

**Subhead (LOCKED):**
EN: `Notice what you'd miss across all your brokers.`
RU: `Замечай то, что упустил бы между брокерами.`

**Primary CTA:**
EN: `Get early access` (not "Sign up", not "Start free trial" — pre-alpha framing)
RU: `Запросить ранний доступ`

**Below-CTA microcopy (the trust line):**
EN: `No card. No connection required to look around. Read-only when you connect.`
RU: `Без карты. Подключение не требуется, чтобы посмотреть. Доступ только на чтение.`

This microcopy is doing more work than it looks. Three friction-killers in one line:
1. No card (kills "is this a trial trap?")
2. Look-around-without-connecting (kills "do I have to hand over my brokerage right now?")
3. Read-only (kills "can it move my money?")

**Word count target:** ~35 words including microcopy.

RU: `Задай тот вопрос, который ты гуглил.`

>

RU:

RU: `Три вещи, которые Provedo замечает и которые вручную почти никто не ловит:`

**Three short numbered cards:**

RU body: `Provedo подсвечивает предстоящие выплаты по всем счетам, до даты отсечения.`

RU header: `Позиция, которая дублируется.`
RU body: `Когда одна и та же экспозиция сидит и в IBKR, и в робо-эдвайзоре — ты это увидишь.`

**3.**
EN header: `Drawdown you'd only notice in April.`

The reader's last objection before signing up is "what is this thing actually doing with my data, and what is it not allowed to do?" This section answers both, in one breath.

RU: `Только чтение. Без советов. Без сюрпризов.`

Standard, but two specific things:

**Required disclaimer (legally locked, must be visible):**

RU: `Provedo предоставляет только анализ и информацию. Не является инвестиционным, финансовым, юридическим или налоговым советом. Все решения по портфелю принимаешь ты.`

## 5. CTA strategy

**One primary action across the entire page: "Get early access"**

Repeated three times: hero, possibly mid-page (after section 4 if scroll telemetry justifies it later), and final CTA. Same wording every time. No "Start now" / "Try free" / "Sign up" variation. Repetition makes the action feel inevitable.

**Supporting actions (all secondary, all subtle):**

1. **"See full institution list" →** in section 3. Low-pressure for the user who needs to verify their broker is supported before committing.
2. **"How does it work?"** — small text link in nav that anchor-scrolls to section 2. Helps the reader who lands without context.
3. **No newsletter signup as a fallback CTA.** It dilutes the primary. Pre-alpha = waitlist is the *primary*, not a consolation prize.

**What I'd reject:**
- "Book a demo" — wrong tone for self-directed retail
- "Watch video" — adds friction, signals we don't trust the page
- "Talk to sales" — we don't have sales, and pretending we do is the corporate-cosplay trap
- A second-tier "free plan" CTA in hero — the page's job is to convert to waitlist, not to teach pricing tiers

---

## 6. What I am explicitly NOT writing

Patterns I'm rejecting on purpose, with the reason for each:

1. **Corporate hedge** — "Provedo *aims to help* investors *better understand*..." Hedging signals lack of conviction. Cut.

2. **Sage-overdose** — "Wisdom for the modern investor. Insights that compound. Clarity in complexity." This tone is now a parody. Every fintech in the last 5 years has tried it. Skip.

3. **Stripe-imperative** — "Build. Ship. Earn." Three-word commands work for developer tools. They don't work for someone deciding whether to trust software with their savings.

4. **Lovable-warm cosplay** — "Hey there ✨ We're Provedo, your friendly portfolio buddy 💪." Self-directed investors with $200k portfolios will close the tab in 4 seconds. Wrong audience match.

5. **Notion-clever** — "What if your portfolio could just... talk back?" Coyness reads as we don't have anything substantive to say.

6. **Robinhood-gamified** — emojis in headers, exclamation marks, "Let's go!" CTAs. Kills credibility instantly with the actual ICP.

7. **Bloomberg-cold** — "Institutional-grade portfolio intelligence for the discerning investor." Pre-alpha pretending to be Bloomberg = embarrassing.

8. **Fake testimonial templates** — "Loved by 10,000+ investors" badges, made-up quotes, fictional names with stock photos. We don't have testimonials yet. Pretending we do is unrecoverable trust damage on day one.

9. **AI-feature-list LARP** — "Powered by GPT-4. Vector embeddings. RAG-enabled." Reader does not care. Reader cares whether it answers the question.

10. **Generic "comprehensive" / "seamless" / "powerful" / "intuitive"** vocabulary. If a word could appear on any SaaS landing page, it should not appear on this one.

## 7. Three reference brand voices

The page should sound like **a blend of** these three. Not a copy of any one.

### 1. Stripe Atlas (NOT Stripe Payments)
**Why:** Stripe Atlas writes for sophisticated readers making consequential decisions. Sentences are short. Claims are specific. There is no salesmanship. The voice respects the reader's intelligence and time. That is the exact register we need.

**What to borrow:** the cadence, the calm, the absence of cheerleading.
**What to leave:** the developer vocabulary.

### 2. Wise (formerly TransferWise)
**Why:** Wise sells a financial product to people who have been burned by financial products. They earn trust by being radically specific about what they do and don't do. "We use the real exchange rate. Here it is. Here's our fee." Provedo is in the same trust posture.

**What to borrow:** the upfront-honesty-as-marketing approach, the willingness to name what we're *not* doing.
**What to leave:** the bright UI tone — Wise is louder visually than we should be.

### 3. Linear (the changelog and marketing site, not the product UI)
**Why:** Linear treats every sentence on its marketing pages as a deliberate choice. Headers are declarations. Body copy never overstays. They sound like adults building software for adults. They never gush about themselves.

**What to borrow:** the editorial discipline, the confidence to say less, the refusal to use marketing-ese.
**What to leave:** the design-aesthete vibe — Provedo is for investors, not product designers.

---

## 8. The risk that worries me most

The biggest risk is that **the locked hero head + sub set up an expectation the rest of the page has to deliver on within one scroll**, and that the page tries to do too much from there. The reader has been told "Provedo will lead you through your portfolio." If section 2 is anything other than an immediate, specific demonstration of *how it leads them through*, the page collapses.

This is why I've structured section 2 as the earner — not a feature list, not "how it works," but a single concrete chat exchange that proves the headline in 5 seconds of looking. If the designer puts a generic dashboard screenshot there instead of a real-looking chat transcript, the page dies in section 2 and nothing in sections 3-6 can recover it.

Recommendation to right-hand: please make sure the design partner reads section 2 of this doc closely. The visual choice for that section is load-bearing for the whole page.
